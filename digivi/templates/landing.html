{% trans "Alternate Wetting Drying (AWD)" %}

{% blocktrans %} Rice is a major crop in Punjab, particularly during the Kharif season, where traditional transplanting methods (TPR) are commonly used. These methods require large quantities of water, typically sourced from groundwater via subsidized electric tubewells, leading to severe aquifer depletion. The transplanted paddy system demands continuous field flooding, especially during the early vegetative and flowering stages, making rice farming highly water-intensive and increasingly unsustainable. Compounding the issue, traditional rice cultivation is also a major contributor to methane emissions, accounting for around 12% of global methane output. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Detailed Explanation of the Research" %}

{% blocktrans %} In response, the Punjab government has introduced cash incentives to promote water-saving techniques like Direct Seeding of Rice (DSR) and micro-irrigation systems such as drip irrigation, though their adoption remains limited. As a more practical solution, researchers and institutions like the International Rice Research Institute (IRRI) advocate for modifying traditional practices through methods like Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD). {% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans %} Under AWD, instead of maintaining continuous flooding, farmers allow water levels to recede up to 5 cm below the soil before re-flooding to a shallow level, repeating this cycle during critical growth stages. This approach can significantly reduce water usage without sacrificing yields. The proposed study aims to evaluate whether payment-for-ecosystem-services (PES) incentives can effectively promote AWD adoption and quantify the actual water savings achieved by participating farmers. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Remote Controllers" %}

{% blocktrans %} In Punjab, rice farming heavily depends on groundwater accessed via submersible pumps powered by state-subsidized electricity. However, the unpredictable 8-hour electricity schedule often forces farmers to keep pumps running continuously, especially during nighttime or rainy conditions, leading to over-irrigation and excessive groundwater extraction. Since there's no direct cost associated with water or electricity, there's little incentive for farmers to limit usage, despite the long-term threat to water sustainability. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Detailed Explanation of the Research" %}

{% blocktrans %} Remote controllers offer a solution by allowing farmers to operate pumps remotely, even during odd hours or adverse weather. These smart systems improve irrigation scheduling, reduce unnecessary power consumption, and enhance convenience by minimizing the need for manual visits. Integrating sensors and automation, they can play a critical role in promoting sustainable water use and boosting farmer satisfaction. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Direct Seeded Rice (DSR)" %}

{% blocktrans %} Direct Seeding of Rice (DSR) is an innovative planting method that eliminates the need for transplanting seedlings, saving water and labor while maintaining high yields. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Detailed Explanation of the Research" %}

{% blocktrans %} DSR reduces water consumption by avoiding continuous flooding and nursery raising. It shortens the crop cycle and allows timely sowing of subsequent crops. Challenges include weed management and the need for precise field leveling. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Water Metering" %}

{% blocktrans %} Water metering provides real-time data on water usage, enabling farmers to optimize irrigation and conserve water resources effectively. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Detailed Explanation of the Research" %}

{% blocktrans %} Accurate water metering helps track consumption, detect leaks, and supports water-saving policies and incentive programs. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Water Level Monitoring" %}

{% blocktrans %} Continuous monitoring of water levels in fields ensures optimal irrigation, preventing waterlogging or drought stress. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Detailed Explanation of the Research" %}

{% blocktrans %} Accurate water level data helps prevent over-irrigation and supports sustainable groundwater management. Integration with automated irrigation systems enhances water use efficiency. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Remote Sensing" %}

{% blocktrans %} Remote sensing technology uses satellite imagery to monitor crop health, soil moisture, and agricultural productivity from a distance. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Detailed Explanation of the Research" %}

{% blocktrans %} By analyzing spectral data, remote sensing helps in early detection of crop stress, optimizing fertilizer application, and improving overall farm management. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Agriculture in Punjab" %}

{% blocktrans %} Punjab, often referred to as the "Granary of India", is one of the most agriculturally prosperous states in the country. The state's agricultural success is largely attributed to the Green Revolution of the 1960s, which introduced high-yielding varieties (HYVs), mechanization, and chemical fertilizers, transforming Punjab into a leading food producer. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Crop residue management" %}

{% blocktrans %} At the end of the rice season farmers use the combine to harvest rice, this leaves hard stubbles at the root of the plant projecting 10-15 cm above surface level. Thereafter, farmers will plough the field with a reaper to cut these stubbles leaving behind straw. {% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans %} The Direct Seeded Rice (DSR) method is an emerging alternative to traditional transplanting. Unlike the transplanting method, DSR involves sowing rice seeds with a tractor-seeder directly into the field without a separate nursery or puddling. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Transplanting Rice" %}

{% blocktrans %} The transplanting rice (TPR) method of rice cultivation has been a traditional and widely practiced technique in Punjab, India, where rice is the major kharif (monsoon) crop. This method involves raising seedlings in a separate nursery and then transplanting them into a puddled, waterlogged field. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Tube-well" %}

{% blocktrans %} Tube-well is by far the most pervasive source of irrigation in the area. It is a bore-well dug under the surface level the depth of which can vary between areas but it is 100 to 400ft, mostly around 200ft. The tube-well is called like this as it is a bore in a tube shape. Its diameter tends to be small, relative to the traditional wells. Diameter is usually 3-6". {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Empowering Farmers, Sustaining Punjab" %}

{% blocktrans %} We are actively engaging with a cohort of 500 farmers across Patiala District as part of a comprehensive field research initiative aimed at improving irrigation practices and understanding on-farm water use in the aim to alleviate farmers' livelihoods and increase their income while maintaining food security in India. Our team conducts regular visits to each farm, where we carry out structured surveys to capture detailed information on farming practices, irrigation behavior, and crop decisions. At the same time, we are systematically measuring groundwater levels and installing water meters to monitor actual water use at the plot level. Each farm is geo-mapped to ensure spatial precision and enable integration with remote sensing and hydrological models. {% endblocktrans %}
